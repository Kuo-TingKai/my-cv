Introduction

In chemistry and biology, “obscure” often means a field with a very high barrier to entry, still at the threshold between theory and application, or not yet fully accepted by mainstream frameworks. This document is not ordered by “depth” or “coldness”; it is organized by thematic families. Within each family, the areas are related by shared concepts (e.g. sustainability, quantum effects, synthetic redesign, information, or extreme conditions), and the relations between them are briefly explained at the start of each family. Terminology is kept accessible so readers with a basic scientific background can map the ideas to their own interests.

I. Green Chemistry, Sustainable Synthesis, and Solvents

Relations within this family
This family centers on replacing toxic or energy-intensive processes with tunable, often bio-compatible alternatives. Deep eutectic solvents (DES) are mixtures of solids (e.g. choline chloride and urea) that form room-temperature liquids; they are cheap, low-toxicity, and biodegradable and are used in metal extraction, biomass processing, and smart drug delivery. Electrobiochemical synthesis bypasses photosynthesis by feeding electrons directly to microbes (e.g. via electrodes) to convert CO₂ into fuels or plastic precursors—a path to zero-carbon factories. Synthetic biomineralization engineers organisms to control inorganic formation (e.g. shells, bone); one aim is carbon-capture cement: bacteria that turn atmospheric CO₂ into calcium carbonate so that building materials “grow” in a carbon-negative way. All three share the theme of circular economy and green chemistry at the interface of chemistry, microbiology, and materials.

Overview
DES are moving from lab curiosity to industrial use as the demand for circular economy grows. Electrobiochemical synthesis can exceed the energy-conversion efficiency of natural photosynthesis and is at an early, high-potential stage. Synthetic biomineralization spans materials science, geology, and bioengineering and could yield new construction and carbon-capture paradigms.

II. Spatial Omics, Sensing, and Digital Olfaction

Relations within this family
This family focuses on where and how biological signals are read—in tissue space or as chemical/sensory data. Spatial omics keeps tissue structure intact while profiling gene expression in situ, so that the “map” of which cell does what and where is preserved; it is critical for tumor microenvironments and neuroscience. Digital olfaction combines chemical sensors, receptor chemistry, and machine learning to digitize smell (e.g. electronic noses for early disease detection or “sending” scent). Vibrational cell signaling uses Raman or infrared spectroscopy to monitor real-time chemistry in living cells without labels—seeing how drugs or metabolites change the cell second by second. All three aim to turn hidden or analog biological information into interpretable, often high-dimensional data.

Overview
Spatial omics is in a growth phase; instruments and data are expensive and complex, but algorithmic progress is making it a pillar of precision medicine. Digital olfaction benefits from better protein-structure prediction and receptor modeling. Vibrational signaling offers label-free, live monitoring for drug development and stem-cell research but faces signal-processing and background-noise challenges.

III. Epigenetic and Morphogenetic Control

Relations within this family
This family is about controlling cell fate and form without changing the DNA sequence. Epigenetic editing (e.g. CRISPR-off) uses chemical modifications (e.g. methylation) to silence or tune genes reversibly; it avoids cutting DNA and is being tested for chronic pain, hepatitis B, and obesity. Morphogenetic fields and bioelectrics study how bioelectric patterns (voltage gradients across cell layers) guide morphogenesis; experiments show that changing these gradients can alter body plan (e.g. extra limbs or heads) without changing genes. Both directions challenge a purely gene-centric view: one acts on the epigenome, the other on electrical patterning as a control layer for growth and regeneration.

Overview
Epigenetic editing is entering clinical trials for chronic disease and offers a more reversible, often ethically smoother alternative to gene editing. Bioelectrics and morphogenetic fields are gaining traction for regeneration and cancer as a problem of “scaling of the cellular self”; key work comes from labs such as Michael Levin’s (Tufts).

IV. Synthetic Biology, Xenobiology, and Code Expansion

Relations within this family
This family extends life’s molecular vocabulary beyond the canonical one. Artificial organelles are designed nanoscale reactors inside living cells to perform tasks that natural organelles do not (e.g. capturing microplastics or synthesizing drugs in situ). Xenobiology builds alternative genetics (e.g. XNA instead of DNA) or non-canonical amino acids so that synthetic organisms cannot exchange genes with natural ones—a biocontainment strategy. Genetic code expansion reassigns stop codons to encode non-natural amino acids (21st, 22nd, etc.), yielding proteins with new chemistries (e.g. photo-switchable, conductive, or metal-binding). Organosilicon xenobiology explores carbon–silicon bonds in living systems (e.g. directed evolution of enzymes that form C–Si bonds), opening “semi-biological” materials for extreme environments or space. Heterochiral biology studies what happens when mirror-image (D/L) life coexists in one cell; hybrid systems could be resistant to natural viruses that recognize only one chirality. The shared theme is expanding the chemical and genetic toolkit of life for containment, materials, or new functions.

Overview
Artificial organelles blur biology and chemical engineering. Xenobiology and code expansion are moving from proof-of-concept to applications in therapeutics and materials. Organosilicon life is at an early stage but relevant to astrobiology and biotechnology. Heterochiral biology may lead to virus-resistant cells or novel chiral chemistry.

V. Quantum and Spin Effects in Biology

Relations within this family
This family asks whether quantum mechanics (coherence, tunneling, entanglement, spin) is harnessed by living systems and how to exploit it. Quantum biology studies phenomena such as radical-pair mechanisms in bird navigation and quantum coherence in photosynthesis. Chiral-induced spin selectivity (CISS) shows that electrons passing through chiral molecules (e.g. DNA helices) are spin-filtered—suggesting room-temperature spin effects in biology and applications in spintronics or chiral catalysis. Molecular spintronics uses single organic molecules (e.g. porphyrins) as spin-based logic elements, with longer spin lifetimes than in metals. Quantum vibrational theory of olfaction proposes that smell detects molecular vibrations (quantum tunneling) rather than shape alone—with implications for a “smell display” industry. Quantum neurobiology explores whether consciousness or memory involves quantum effects in microtubules. Biological Bose–Einstein condensates speculate about coherent quantum-like states in membranes or protein networks at room temperature. The family is linked by quantum and spin physics in warm, wet, biological settings.

Overview
Quantum biology and CISS are the most developed; CISS has clear experimental support and applications in spin filters and catalysis. Molecular spintronics aims at molecular-scale, low-power logic. Quantum olfaction and quantum consciousness remain controversial but motivate precise experiments. Biological BEC is highly speculative and faces strong skepticism from mainstream physics.

VI. Phase Separation, Dynamic Covalent Chemistry, and In Vivo Chemistry

Relations within this family
This family deals with reversible organization and non-classical reaction environments. Liquid–liquid phase separation (LLPS) describes membrane-less condensates (liquid droplets) inside cells; many diseases (e.g. ALS, dementia) are linked to droplets that solidify or aggregate. Dynamic covalent chemistry (DCC) uses reversible bonds so that molecules can “heal” or adapt; applications include self-healing coatings and smart sensors that recognize analytes by molecular reorganization. In vivo sonochemical synthesis uses ultrasound (cavitation) to drive chemistry inside living tissue—e.g. triggering drug release or synthesis at a tumor site non-invasively. All three emphasize tunable, responsive chemical organization rather than static lock-and-key binding.

Overview
LLPS has become a major theme in cell biology and drug discovery (“phase-transition drugs”). DCC is established in materials and sensing. In vivo sonochemistry is niche but offers a non-invasive way to localize chemistry in the body.

In vivo sonochemical synthesisUltrasound-driven chemistry inside living tissueVery highNon-invasive, localized drug activation

VII. Molecular Communication, Information Thermodynamics, and Non-Equilibrium Life

Relations within this family
This family treats molecules and cells as information channels or thermodynamic engines. Molecular communication (nanonetworks) models chemical diffusion as data transmission (Shannon-style), with applications to in-body “internet of things” where nanodevices communicate via chemical packets instead of radio. Information-to-energy physico-chemistry (Landauer’s principle) studies how erasing or writing information in the cell (e.g. DNA repair, protein synthesis) couples to heat and work—with potential for “information metabolism” and energy-from-data devices. Non-equilibrium biological thermodynamics describes life as open, dissipative systems that maintain order by consuming energy and exporting entropy; it aims at predictive models for critical transitions (e.g. multi-organ failure, aging). The shared idea is life as an information- and energy-processing system that can be formalized and engineered.

Overview
Molecular communication is at the interface of communications engineering and chemistry. Information thermodynamics connects statistical physics and biology. Non-equilibrium thermodynamics requires strong mathematical background (statistical physics, stochastic processes) but could yield universal principles for robustness and collapse.

VIII. Structured Water, LENR, and Controversial Edge

Relations within this family
This family groups highly contested or paradigm-challenging ideas. Structured / fourth-phase water (EZ water) proposes that water near biological surfaces forms an “exclusion zone” with ordered, crystal-like structure—potentially relevant to energy conversion and protein folding. Biological transmutations / low-energy nuclear reactions (LENR) in biology suggest that living systems might mediate low-temperature nuclear transformations (e.g. potassium to calcium); evidence is disputed and the field is often labeled pseudoscience, though a few groups pursue careful experiments. Both sit at the border of mainstream acceptance and require rigorous replication and theory.

Overview
Fourth-phase water has a dedicated following (e.g. Pollack’s work) but is not widely accepted in physics. Biological LENR is extremely controversial; if ever confirmed, it would reshape nuclear and biochemical boundaries. Readers should treat these as high-risk, high-uncertainty frontiers.

Structured / fourth-phase water (EZ)Ordered water at interfaces; “biological water”Very high (controversial)Energy conversion, protein folding, batteries
Biological transmutations / LENRLow-temperature nuclear change in living systemsVery high (controversial)New energy paradigm or disproof

IX. Biosemiotics, Dark Matter, and Shadow Biosphere

Relations within this family
This family extends biology into meaning, the unknown, and alternative life. Biosemiotics studies life as “semiosis”—cells as interpreters of signs rather than mere chemical reactors; it could inform “semiotic drugs” that persuade cells to repair rather than blunt inhibition. Metabolic and biological dark matter refers to the large fraction of sequences (e.g. 40–90% in some environments) that do not match any known organism; unlocking this “dark” chemistry could reveal new enzymes and the possibility of a shadow biosphere—life on Earth with a different molecular logic (e.g. arsenic instead of phosphorus, or a different genetic code). The unifying theme is life as interpretation and unknown or alternative biochemistry.

Overview
Biosemiotics is more conceptual but gains traction as AI and protein “language” models grow. Dark matter and shadow biosphere require culture-independent methods and new detection paradigms; they are central to astrobiology and the search for non-standard life.

X. Neuromorphic and Chemical Computing

Relations within this family
This family builds computation from chemistry and ions rather than silicon. Neuromorphic electrochemistry (ionotronics) uses ion flows in solution to mimic synaptic transmission, aiming at wetware computers that run in liquid with minimal energy. Chemo-predictive neuromorphic engineering couples reaction–diffusion systems (e.g. Belousov–Zhabotinsky oscillators) to decision-making—e.g. chemical “logic gates” or autonomous micro-machines that navigate by concentration gradients. Both seek brain-like or autonomous behavior from chemical dynamics alone.

Overview
Ionotronic and wetware computing are early-stage but align with interest in low-power, embodied computation. B–Z and related oscillators are well-studied; their integration into controllable neuromorphic or robotic systems is an open engineering challenge.

XI. Topology, Time, and Extreme Environments

Relations within this family
This family links structure, time, and extreme conditions. Topological biochemistry studies knots and entanglement in DNA/proteins and aims at “topological drugs” that alter function by changing topology rather than binding. Ultra-fast chronobiology looks at sub-second coordination of reactions inside the cell—many diseases may be “timing” rather than composition errors. Super-pressure biochemistry studies chemistry at millions of atmospheres (e.g. Jupiter-like interiors), where hydrogen metallizes and new compounds form. Directed evolution in astrochemistry evolves terrestrial organisms in non-Earth conditions (e.g. liquid methane, extreme acidity) for space exploration. Paleobiochemistry and resurrection chemistry reconstruct and synthesize ancient proteins to recover lost stability and activity. Nuclidic biology studies how isotope substitution (e.g. deuterium) affects metabolism and lifespan. Mesonic / muonic biology explores chemistry with muons instead of electrons (e.g. muon-catalyzed fusion, ultra-compact “muonic” molecules). Higher-dimensional molecular topology uses manifolds to model folding and binding in higher dimensions to find “dark” drug targets. The family spans geometry of molecules, timing of reactions, and chemistry under extremes.

Overview
Topological biochemistry and chronobiology are emerging; the rest are highly specialized. Astrochemistry-directed evolution and resurrection chemistry have clear long-term goals (space, industrial catalysts). Nuclidic and mesonic biology are niche and resource-intensive; information thermodynamics and Landauer-style ideas connect to some of these themes.

XII. Bioorthogonal Neurochemistry

Relations within this family
This area focuses on precise, non-interfering chemical intervention in the brain. Bioorthogonal neuro-chemistry implants artificial “click” receptors in neurons that do not respond to native neurotransmitters; then, exogenous molecules can selectively toggle specific circuits (e.g. addiction pathways) without broad side effects. It is a frontier of precision neuropharmacology.

Overview
Bioorthogonal chemistry is well established in labeling and drug delivery; its extension to neural circuits is at an early, high-potential stage and could yield far more precise psychopharmacology.

Bioorthogonal neuro-chemistryClick receptors in brain; exogenous control of specific circuitsVery highAddiction, mood, precision psychiatry
