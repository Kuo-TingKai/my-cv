BETSE Bioelectric Simulation: A Learning Note & Glossary

Structured notes and key terms for the Bioelectric Tissue Simulation Engine: ions, channels, DEC, and workflow.

Structured learning notes and a concise glossary of domain terms around BETSE (Bioelectric Tissue Simulation Engine)—for reading the docs and papers with less friction.

1. What BETSE Is and Why the Terms Matter

BETSE is an open-source, cross-platform 2D multiphysics simulator for life-sciences problems: electrodiffusion, ion channels, gene regulatory and biochemical reaction networks. It uses discrete exterior calculus (DEC) on a mesh to evolve voltages, ion concentrations, and flows in tissue. The vocabulary below is the same one used in the codebase, config files, and the Pietak–Levin papers; having a compact reference in learning-note form makes it easier to follow tutorials and supplements.

Takeaway: Treat this note as a cheat sheet while you run betse try, read the YAML options, or skim the validation sections in the papers.

2. Core Simulation Concepts

TermMeaning (learning-note style)
BETSEBioelectric Tissue Simulation Engine: 2D DEC-based simulator for ions, channels, gap junctions, and (optionally) GRNs and metabolism.
Discrete Exterior Calculus (DEC)A mathematical framework on a discrete grid (mesh) that handles differential forms—BETSE uses it to compute potentials, concentrations, and flows in space and time.
Multiphysics simulationCoupling several physical processes in one run (e.g. electricity, diffusion, osmosis, reactions).
2D cellular collectiveA sheet of cells in 2D geometry, connected by gap junctions; BETSE models this simplified tissue.
YAML configurationHuman-readable config files (ions, channel types, boundaries, etc.) that drive the simulation.
Bitmask imageAn image whose pixel values define regions (e.g. cell cluster shape, channel regions); BETSE can import these to set geometry and local properties.

3. Ions, Potentials, and Transport

TermMeaning
Bioelectric / bioelectricalElectrical phenomena in living tissue arising from ion distributions and membrane potentials; tightly linked to development, regeneration, and wound healing.
ElectrodiffusionTransport of ions driven by both concentration gradient and electric field (diffusion + electromigration).
Electro-osmosisFlow of a charged fluid (e.g. extracellular solution) driven by an applied electric field.
Resting potentialSteady transmembrane voltage when the cell is unstimulated; maintained by channels and pumps.
Membrane permeabilityHow easily a given ion or molecule crosses the membrane; affects flux and potential.
Ion concentrationConcentration of an ion inside or outside the cell; in BETSE it varies in time and space.
Transepithelial potential (TEP / TEPD)Steady voltage difference across an epithelial layer; reflects polarized distribution of pumps and channels.
Local field potentialAggregate electrical activity of a local group of cells; used in neural or epithelial measurements and in BETSE.

Ions BETSE can simulate: Na⁺, K⁺, Cl⁻, Ca²⁺, H⁺, anionic protein (P⁻), HCO₃⁻, and others as configured.

4. Channels, Pumps, and Cell–Cell Coupling

TermMeaning
Voltage-gated ion channelChannel whose open/closed state depends on membrane voltage; basis of action potentials and many bioelectric patterns. BETSE supports several in Hodgkin–Huxley form.
Hodgkin–Huxley (HH) formalismClassic model of channel gating with state variables (e.g. m, n, h); parameters often from resources like Channelpedia.
Ion pumpProtein that actively transports ions across the membrane using ATP (e.g. Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase), maintaining gradients.
Ion exchangerMembrane protein that uses one ion’s gradient to drive another ion in the opposite direction (no direct ATP).
Gap junctionPore between adjacent cells allowing ions and small molecules to pass; creates electrical and metabolic coupling.
Tight junctionSealing structure at epithelial cell edges; limits paracellular flow and helps maintain TEP.
Ephaptic couplingCell–cell electrical interaction via the extracellular field rather than via gap junctions.

BETSE allows configuring channel types (e.g. HCN1/2/4, L/T/P/Q-type Ca²⁺, Kir2.1, Kv, Nav, leak, Ca²⁺-gated K⁺) and pumps/exchangers (Ca²⁺-ATPase, Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase, V-ATPase, H⁺/K⁺-ATPase, etc.).

5. Morphology and Guidance

TermMeaning
GalvanotaxisMigration or polarization of cells/tissue along an applied or endogenous electric field; relevant to wound healing, regeneration, and axis formation.
MorphogenesisThe emergence and maintenance of organism shape and structure; BETSE-related work often asks how bioelectric patterns instruct morphology.
Positional informationSignals (which can include bioelectric gradients) that tell a cell “where it is” in the tissue and influence fate and form.

6. Gene and Biochemical Networks

TermMeaning
Gene regulatory network (GRN)Network of genes regulating each other via transcription factors and signaling molecules; BETSE’s sim-grn subcommand simulates such networks.
Biochemical reaction networkNetwork of metabolic and signaling reactions; can be modeled at cellular or mitochondrial level and integrated with channels and gap junctions in BETSE.
MetabolismNetwork of material and energy conversion in the cell; in BETSE it can be coupled to bioelectric and ion concentration dynamics.

7. BETSE Workflow (CLI Terms)

TermMeaning
SeedPhase that generates the initial cell cluster geometry (e.g. from config or randomness).
Init (initialization)Computes steady-state concentrations and potentials on the seeded geometry as the starting point for time evolution.
Sim (simulation)Time-stepping run of the full bioelectric (and optional GRN/reaction) simulation.
ConfigCreating or pointing to the YAML config file for a run.
PlotExporting results from seed/init/sim as figures, animations, or CSV.
Headless modeNo GUI; compute and write outputs only (e.g. for servers or batch runs).

8. Validation and Use Cases (from the docs)

Typical validation/application contexts mentioned in BETSE papers and docs:

  • Transmembrane voltage changes — How membrane potential responds to single-cell state or environmental ion concentration changes.
  • Transepithelial potential difference (TEPD) — Comparing predicted TEP with experiments.
  • Bioelectrical signals at wound sites — Large-scale wound fields and repair.
